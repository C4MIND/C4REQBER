## 2. Literature Review

The escalating trajectory of global mean surface temperature toward the 2°C threshold above pre-industrial levels, as established by the Paris Agreement, has prompted serious consideration of solar radiation modification (SRM) techniques as potential supplementary interventions to emissions reduction and carbon dioxide removal. Among proposed SRM methods, Marine Cloud Brightening (MCB)—the intentional injection of sea-salt aerosols into shallow marine stratocumulus clouds to increase their albedo—has attracted sustained scientific attention due to its theoretical potential for targeted, regionally-adaptive radiative forcing [2]. This literature review synthesizes current knowledge on MCB efficacy, regional implications, and methodological limitations, identifying critical knowledge gaps that motivate the proposed research.

**Theoretical Foundations and Global-Scale Efficacy**

The foundational mechanism of MCB relies on the Twomey effect, wherein increasing cloud condensation nuclei (CCN) concentrations enhances cloud droplet number concentration, thereby increasing cloud albedo for fixed liquid water content [2]. Latham [2] provided the seminal framework for MCB, proposing that a 3–5% increase in cloud albedo over 10–20% of global marine stratocumulus areas could offset a doubling of atmospheric CO₂. Subsequent global circulation model (GCM) studies have largely confirmed that MCB can produce substantial negative radiative forcing, though estimates vary considerably. Jones [5] compared the climate impacts of MCB with stratospheric aerosol injection (SAI), finding that while both methods could mitigate global mean temperature increases, MCB produced distinct spatial patterns of cooling with weaker polar amplification reduction relative to SAI. This differential regional response raises important questions about the suitability of MCB for preserving cryospheric stability.

Kim [4] extended these analyses by examining the controllability of MCB under different emission scenarios, demonstrating that the radiative forcing from MCB is sensitive to background aerosol concentrations and cloud microphysical regimes. The authors proposed that MCB may exhibit nonlinear responses to aerosol injection rates, complicating efforts to achieve precise temperature targets. Stuart [7] further contributed a critical perspective, reporting reduced efficacy of MCB under future climate conditions due to changes in cloud regime distributions and increased atmospheric stability. This finding suggests that the effectiveness of MCB may diminish precisely when its deployment is most needed, a phenomenon that warrants systematic investigation.

**Regional Dynamics and Polar Implications**

The capacity of MCB to prevent 2°C warming requires not only global mean temperature reduction but also the preservation of climate-sensitive regions, particularly the polar cryosphere. Duffey [3] reviewed solar geoengineering in polar regions, highlighting that SRM methods exhibit considerable spatial heterogeneity in their ability to reduce high-latitude warming. The review identified that MCB, when deployed in mid-latitude regions, may produce limited polar cooling relative to SAI due to the absence of direct aerosol-cloud interactions over polar marine areas and the dominance of poleward heat transport mechanisms. This regional inefficacy raises concerns about the ability of MCB to mitigate polar amplification and its consequences, including accelerated ice-sheet mass loss and sea-level rise.

Hirasawa [3] emphasized the need for accelerated exploration of MCB impacts beyond global mean metrics, specifically calling for high-resolution assessments of regional climate responses. The authors argued that GCM-based assessments of MCB have predominantly focused on radiative forcing and global mean temperature, leaving substantial uncertainty regarding regional hydrological cycle changes, sea-ice dynamics, and the stability of polar ice sheets. Critically, no published study has systematically evaluated the potential for MCB to influence Antarctic ice-sheet dynamics or the associated contribution to sea-level rise, representing a significant gap in the literature.

**Aerosol-Cloud Interactions and Observational Constraints**

Recent observational advances have begun to constrain the fundamental aerosol-cloud processes underlying MCB. Zang [6] provided empirical evidence from satellite and in situ measurements demonstrating that fine and coarse aerosol particles differentially affect marine cloud microphysics and albedo. Their findings indicate that the efficacy of MCB is strongly modulated by background aerosol concentrations and cloud thermodynamic phase, with coarse-mode aerosols exhibiting reduced albedo enhancement efficiency under pristine conditions. This observation aligns with the theoretical expectation that optimal CCN concentrations for cloud brightening exist within a narrow range, beyond which additional aerosols may suppress precipitation without further increasing albedo.

Stuart [7] further demonstrated that the efficacy of MCB is regime-dependent, with closed-cell stratocumulus clouds showing greater brightening potential than open-cell or cumuliform clouds. These regime-dependent responses complicate the scaling of local cloud brightening effects to global climate impacts and underscore the need for process-level understanding that can only be achieved through field experiments at appropriate spatial scales.

**Gaps in the Literature**

Despite progress in understanding MCB mechanisms and global-scale impacts, three critical knowledge gaps remain that directly constrain the scientific basis for deploying MCB to prevent 2°C warming.

*Gap 1: Regional efficacy and uncertainty in polar regions.* The capacity of MCB to protect polar cryosphere and mitigate sea-level rise from Antarctic ice sheets remains unquantified. Existing GCM studies [4, 5] have focused on global mean temperature and mid-latitude responses, while polar-specific assessments are limited to SAI comparisons [3]. The winter-time climate impacts of MCB, when polar cloud regimes differ fundamentally from summer conditions, have not been systematically investigated. This gap is critical because Antarctic ice-sheet stability and associated sea-level rise are governed by ocean heat transport and atmospheric circulation patterns that may respond nonlinearly to MCB forcing.

*Gap 2: Systematic assessment of tipping point risks.* The potential for MCB to either mitigate or inadvertently trigger climate tipping points—including Arctic sea-ice loss, Greenland and Antarctic ice-sheet destabilization, and Amazon dieback—has not been evaluated using computationally efficient methods that allow exploration of large intervention parameter spaces. Current GCM-based assessments [5, 7] are computationally prohibitive for comprehensive sensitivity studies, leaving the risk landscape of MCB deployment poorly characterized.

*Gap 3: Validation at intermediate spatial scales.* The community lacks experimental validation of MCB cloud albedo enhancement at the ~100×100 km spatial scale that bridges high-resolution large-eddy simulations and GCM parameterizations. Zang [6] and Stuart [8] provide observational constraints from natural variability, but controlled field experiments at this scale are absent. Such experiments are essential to reduce uncertainties in aerosol-cloud interactions and to provide empirical constraints for model development.

**Synthesis**

The literature demonstrates that MCB possesses theoretical potential to contribute to preventing 2°C warming, but substantial uncertainties regarding regional efficacy, tipping-point interactions, and process-level validation remain unresolved. The proposed research directly addresses these gaps through a three-part investigation: (1) high-resolution coupled climate-ice sheet modeling to assess MCB impacts on polar climate and sea-level rise, (2) emulator-based exploration of MCB parameter spaces for tipping point risk assessment, and (3) a proposed limited-area field experiment design for intermediate-scale validation. Formal verification only checks logical consistency, not physical realism; thus, SAT results do not guarantee that MCB will work in the real world.

These limitations necessitate cautious interpretation and a multi-model ensemble approach in future work.
