### 2.2 Materials Resilience Under Compact Reactor Conditions

The material challenges for commercial fusion deployment are well documented. Quadling [3] identifies key issues including neutron-induced swelling, tritium permeation, and transmutation product buildup in structural materials. These problems are exacerbated in compact reactor designs, which feature higher neutron flux (proposed >2 MW/m²) and smaller shielding volumes, leading to accelerated damage rates. The author emphasizes the need for validated testing protocols that simulate the combined effects of 14.1 MeV neutron irradiation, thermal cycling, and tritium exposure.

Feder [6] revives interest in waste transmutation applications of fusion, noting that high-flux environments could simultaneously reduce long-lived nuclear waste while producing energy. This dual-purpose capability is hypothesized to improve the economic case for compact reactors, but it imposes additional material constraints—specifically, the need for alloys that can withstand both fusion neutron damage and transmutation-induced compositional changes.

The literature reveals no experimental data on material performance under the specific flux conditions predicted for compact reactors (proposed as 5–10 times higher than ITER). Furthermore, existing testing protocols, such as those using fission reactors or ion beams, cannot replicate the simultaneous production of helium and hydrogen from neutron-induced reactions. This gap is critical because material failure under high-flux conditions could negate the advantages of compact designs. The proposed research will investigate the use of triple-beam irradiation (heavy ions, helium, and hydrogen) to simulate transmutation damage, combined with post-irradiation tritium permeation measurements.
