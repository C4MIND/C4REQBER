The loss of soil organic carbon (SOC) is both a driver and a consequence of desertification. This perspective is extended by Unknown [2], who proposes that anthropogenic desertification can be addressed through engineering approaches that restore soil function. The proposed mechanism is that SOC depletion reduces soil structure, water-holding capacity, and nutrient cycling, creating a positive feedback loop that accelerates desertification. However, the specific microbial pathways linking SOC loss to desertification onset remain poorly characterized across different climatic zones.

Gama [3] provides a comprehensive overview of soils' role in sustainability and climate change, emphasizing that SOC constitutes the largest terrestrial carbon pool. The review highlights that even modest increases in SOC could significantly offset anthropogenic CO₂ emissions, but it stops short of specifying the microbial mechanisms that govern carbon stabilization versus mineralization. This mechanistic gap is critical because without understanding how microbial communities process carbon inputs under varying environmental conditions, predictions of sequestration potential remain speculative.

### 2.2 Regenerative Agriculture and Carbon Sequestration

Regenerative agriculture encompasses a suite of practices—including no-till farming, cover cropping, agroforestry, and rotational grazing—designed to enhance SOC while improving ecosystem services. Tan [7] synthesizes evidence from multiple studies, reporting that regenerative practices can increase SOC stocks by 0.3–1.0 Mg C ha⁻¹ year⁻¹, with variation depending on climate, soil type, and management history. However, the synthesis reveals high uncertainty in outcomes, which the author attributes to inconsistent methodological approaches and the absence of standardized measurement protocols.

Encarnation [8] offers a more nuanced perspective by examining trade-offs and win-win scenarios in regenerative agriculture. The study finds that while SOC sequestration is generally positive, outcomes are context-dependent; for example, increased carbon inputs from cover crops may be offset by higher greenhouse gas emissions from decomposition under certain moisture regimes. This suggests that regenerative practices alone may not guarantee desertification reversal without targeted microbial management.

Pathirana [2] introduces a genetic resources perspective, arguing that plant genetic diversity is essential for developing crops adapted to degraded soils. This aligns with the concept that aboveground biomass composition directly influences belowground carbon dynamics. However, the study does not quantify how different plant traits—such as root architecture or exudate chemistry—affect microbial community assembly and subsequent carbon stabilization.

### 3.4 Epistemic Limitations
